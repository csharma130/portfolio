Neonatal Surfactant Delivery Device

Problem Statement: Physicians at the UC Davis Medical Center identified that the tools used to perform Less Invasive Surfactant Administration (LISA) on neonatal patients impose excessive mechanical stress on the tracheal tissue and lack the maneuverability required to navigate the narrow and delicate anatomy of the neonatal airway — creating a clinical need for a less traumatic, more controllable delivery solution.

What Was Done: Led development of a neonatal surfactant delivery catheter as part of the UC Davis Biomedical Engineering senior design program.

How?: The process began with a hands-on observation alongside a physician to directly understand their clinical concerns. From there, the team developed a survey to capture structured user needs data, and conducted a thorough state-of-the-art review of existing devices — evaluating competing solutions across dimensions such as cost, dimensions, materials, intended users, and commercial availability. Multiple design concepts were generated and evaluated before converging on a single direction. The team then worked through material selection, manufacturing method considerations, and developed a prototype incorporating physician-recommended features including enhanced maneuverability, depth visibility markers, and a softer durometer to reduce tracheal trauma. My primary contributions were in FMEA and V&V testing — identifying failure modes and their potential clinical impact, and designing and executing verification and validation tests to assess whether the prototype met its intended design requirements. As no ISO standard exists specifically for intratracheal catheters, V&V testing was developed by borrowing applicable testing requirements from ISO 10555 (intravascular catheters) as the closest relevant standard — a practical exercise in engineering judgment under regulatory ambiguity.

New Process Recharacterization

Problem Statement: Within the first three months of a new production line launch, 33% of lots either failed or were flagged — driven by individual samples exhibiting a biological concentration at or below half that of the overall lot, a critical deviation from acceptable uniformity thresholds.

What Was Done: Led a thorough, multi-year root cause analysis investigating 20 distinct process variables — including, but not limited to, impeller design, mixing speed, dispense rate, and positional instability.

How?: For each variable, I authored traceable, explicitly defined engineering tests in a lab notebook, executed them in a controlled manner — typically isolating one variable at a time — and submitted samples to QA for results. Upon receiving results, I performed statistical analysis in Minitab to determine, through data-driven conclusions, whether the variable under investigation was a contributing factor to the observed failures. As each test was unique, the preparation required varied considerably. Some tests required me to design and fabricate positional fixtures using rapid prototyping, while others required collecting aliquots in test tubes at incremental time intervals to capture process behavior at each stage. This methodical approach ultimately revealed that the failures were driven by multiple root causes rather than a single variable — a finding that would not have been possible without the systematic, one-variable-at-a-time investigative approach.

03
Upgraded Autoclave Rack

Problem Statement: An existing autoclave cycle was rendered non-operational due to bottles tipping within the original rack design, limiting the number of viable use cases and creating a recurring bottleneck in production throughput.

What Was Done: Designed and implemented an upgraded autoclave rack to resolve instability issues and expand cycle compatibility. The new design increased bottle retention and positional stability across multiple cycle configurations, restoring full operability to the affected cycle and broadening the rack's overall use cases within the manufacturing environment.

How?: Began by characterizing design constraints through precise measurement of the existing autoclave rack dimensions using a high-fidelity ruler. Using a legacy rack — no longer sold by the original manufacturer but still physically available — as the design reference, I measured hole diameters and center-to-center spacing using calipers, catalogued the total hole count, and calculated available surface area to ensure adequate steam penetration. All findings were translated into a new design in SolidWorks, where I incorporated properly sized structural beams to eliminate the beam deflection observed in the legacy rack. The final design was sent to a third-party vendor for machining in stainless steel and subsequently passivated to meet material and cleanliness requirements for use in a GMP environment. Design verification was performed by mounting thermocouples at the same positions used during the original autoclave validation. To ensure worst-case coverage, the most challenging cycle was selected — the one that drives the autoclave to its highest temperature, with the slowest exhaust time and longest exposure duration — then confirming that temperatures at each thermocouple location remained within specification as defined by the original validation protocol.

Results: Increased autoclave usage cases by 20% and eliminated beam deflection by 100%.
