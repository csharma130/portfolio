Neonatal Surfactant Delivery Device

Problem Statement: Physicians at UC Davis Medical Center found that existing tools for Less Invasive Surfactant Administration (LISA) were too rigid and hard to maneuver in the neonatal airway, causing unnecessary tracheal trauma.

What Was Done: Led development of a neonatal surfactant delivery catheter as part of the UC Davis Biomedical Engineering senior design program.

How?: The project started with a direct observation of the LISA procedure alongside a physician to understand the clinical pain points firsthand. From there, the team built a structured user needs survey, followed by a state-of-the-art review that benchmarked competing devices across cost, dimensions, materials, intended users, and market availability. After generating and narrowing down multiple design concepts, the team developed a prototype built around three physician-specified requirements: better maneuverability, depth visibility markers, and a softer durometer. Work spanned the full product development cycle, including material selection, manufacturing method evaluation, FMEA, and V&V testing. Since no ISO standard exists for intratracheal catheters specifically, testing requirements were adapted from ISO 10555 (intravascular catheters) as the closest applicable standard, which required deliberate engineering judgment in interpreting and applying those requirements to a different anatomical context.

New Process Recharacterization

Problem Statement: Within the first three months of a new production line launch, 33% of lots failed or were flagged. The failure criterion: any sample with a biological concentration at or below half the lot average. The failure rate pointed to a systemic process issue.

What Was Done: Conducted a multi-year root cause investigation covering 20 distinct process variables, including impeller design, mixing speed, dispense rate, and positional instability, among others.

How?: Each variable was tested individually. Before running a test, I wrote out the full test protocol in a lab notebook with traceable, explicitly defined steps. After execution, samples were submitted to QA and the results were analyzed in Minitab to determine whether that variable had a statistically meaningful effect on the failure mode. Test preparation varied significantly depending on the variable being studied. Some required designing and fabricating positional fixtures via rapid prototyping; others involved collecting timed aliquots in test tubes at incremental intervals to observe process behavior over time. The investigation ultimately revealed that the failures stemmed from multiple root causes working in combination, not a single variable. That finding would not have surfaced without the disciplined, one-variable-at-a-time approach.

Upgraded Autoclave Rack

Problem Statement: Bottles tipping inside the original rack design rendered one autoclave cycle non-operational, eliminating it from production use entirely.

What Was Done: Designed and built a new autoclave rack that resolved the tipping issue and restored full operability to the affected cycle. The new design also expanded compatibility across additional cycle configurations, increasing the rack's overall utility within the manufacturing environment.

How?: Started by measuring the existing rack dimensions using a high-fidelity ruler to define design constraints. A legacy rack, no longer available from the original manufacturer but still physically on hand, served as the reference. I used calipers to measure hole diameters and center-to-center spacing, documented the total hole count, and calculated available surface area to confirm adequate steam penetration in the new design. Everything was modeled in SolidWorks, with properly sized structural beams incorporated to address the beam deflection present in the legacy design. The finished design was sent to a third-party vendor for machining in stainless steel and then passivated to meet GMP material and cleanliness requirements. For verification, thermocouples were mounted at the same positions used during the original autoclave validation study, and the worst-case cycle was selected for testing: the one with the highest temperature, slowest exhaust time, and longest exposure duration. Temperatures at each location were confirmed to be within specification per the original validation protocol.

Results: Increased autoclave usage cases by 20% and eliminated beam deflection by 100%.
